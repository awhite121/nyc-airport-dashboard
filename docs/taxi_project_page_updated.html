Project Overview

The Problem

Airport taxi planning is not just about the average ETA. The real risk is the slow-tail trip: the ride that looks normal when booked but becomes unusually delayed because of time of day, pickup zone, airport route, or congestion.

This project answers two connected questions for Manhattan → JFK/LGA trips: how long will the ride take? and how likely is it to be unusually slow?

The goal was to turn ML outputs into a decision tool: not just a predicted duration, but a recommended departure buffer based on risk tolerance.

Data Setup

Building a Realistic Dataset

  • Started from 3,574,091 NYC taxi records in the uploaded raw extract.
  • Joined TLC taxi zone lookup to identify boroughs and airport zones.
  • Filtered to Manhattan pickups and JFK/LGA dropoffs.
  • Removed impossible or unstable records such as zero-distance trips and invalid durations.
  • Engineered pickup hour, day of week, airport destination, pickup location, distance, passenger count, and payment type.
Final clean modeling dataset: 43,079 Manhattan → airport trips.
Trip duration varies sharply by pickup hour and airport destination.
Late-risk increases during higher-uncertainty travel windows.

Classification

Predicting Late Risk

A trip was labeled late if it took more than 120% of the typical duration for similar airport, pickup-hour, and day-of-week conditions. This targets the unusually slow tail, not naturally long JFK rides.

Buffer Advisor

Turning Predictions into Action

The final layer translates predicted duration and late-risk into a leave-by recommendation. Instead of showing only an ETA, the advisor can recommend a buffer based on how much late-arrival risk a traveler is willing to accept.

  • Low predicted risk → small buffer.
  • Medium risk → add 10–15 minutes.
  • High risk → add 20–25 minutes before desired arrival.
This is the product layer: model output becomes a human decision, not just a metric.
Adding buffer time sharply reduces predicted late-arrival risk.
